## Project 01: Understanding The Operational Challenge

### Overview
This project focused on building a foundational understanding of how roles are structured within a fulfillment center and how work flows across those roles.

To begin the analysis, I created a workforce structure table outlining key roles, responsibilities, and how each role connects to the broader operation. I then translated that structure into a visual role map to better represent how work moves through the fulfillment center and where operational pressure may build.

This project served as the foundation for the larger attrition analysis by helping identify how workflow design, role responsibilities, and support systems may influence employee experience.

---

### Objective
To understand the operational structure of a fulfillment center and identify how role design, workflow dependencies, and points of failure may contribute to workforce challenges and attrition risk.

---

### What Was Done
- Developed a workforce structure table to define roles and responsibilities  
- Identified how each role contributes to the overall fulfillment process  
- Mapped how work flows across frontline, supervisory, and management roles  
- Created a visual role map to illustrate workflow and role relationships  
- Produced a briefing note analyzing workforce challenges and possible attrition drivers  

---

### Key Questions Explored
- How is work distributed across different roles in a fulfillment center?  
- Where in the fulfillment workflow are errors most likely to originate?  
- How do different roles contribute to operational accuracy and efficiency?  
- What points in the process act as quality control versus points of failure?  
- How might role pressure, workload, and communication gaps contribute to attrition?  

---

### Initial Insights
One of the main takeaways from this project is that errors in fulfillment are often not isolated to a single step. Instead, they can originate early in the process, such as during stowing, and carry through downstream if not caught.

This highlights the importance of upstream accuracy. Later stages in the process often function more as detection points than prevention points, meaning that early workflow issues can create additional pressure across the operation.

Early analysis of workforce conditions also suggests that repetitive tasks, physical strain, unclear communication, and limited support may contribute to higher attrition risk. When these issues compound, they can create a cycle where increased workload leads to stress, burnout, and further turnover.

### Reflection Connection
This project gave me a clearer understanding of how operational workflows function in a fulfillment environment.

What stood out most is how interconnected each layer of the operation is. While the process relies heavily on frontline execution, frontline roles also depend on leadership, communication, and support structures to function effectively.

Mapping the workforce structure helped me see that performance is not driven only by individual tasks. It is shaped by how well each role, tool, workflow, and support network connects across the system.

One area I want to explore further is the role of leadership. My initial briefing note focused more on improving frontline conditions, but this project helped me see that leadership structure, communication, and support may play an equally important role in both performance and attrition.

More of this thinking is captured in the Project 01 [Reflection](reflections/Project-01-Reflection.md).
